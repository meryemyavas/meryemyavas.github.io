Research

Work in Progress

  • Chronic Pain and Occupation Choice
    With Nicholas Garvey

    Chronic pain affects over 50 million U.S. adults and shapes labor supply and retirement decisions. Using the Wisconsin Longitudinal Study, we show that pain is more prevalent non-college graduates, and is especially concentrated in occupations with low abstract task content — such as healthcare support, construction, and machine operation — while executives and professionals face much lower risk. Mid-life pain onset accelerates retirement, suggesting important consequences for retirement policy. Motivated by these findings, we develop a life-cycle directed search model with occupation-specific wages, stochastic pain processes, and endogenous retirement. Estimation of the model is ongoing, with the goal of quantifying the welfare costs of chronic pain and assessing the distributional consequences of retirement policy reforms.

  • Direct Admissions into Majors and College Choice [slides]
    2nd Year Field Paper
    Presented at: 2025 Midwest Theory Conference
    This paper models the trend of direct admissions into competitive programs, such as business and engineering majors, using a model of admissions and enrollment decisions for schools with known and students with unknown heterogeneous quality. Given the preference for competitive majors, students make enrollment decisions, and schools make admissions decisions using noisy signals on student quality. Students weigh their preference for a better university against the likelihood of eventual admission into each university’s competitive major. Universities can use direct admission to skew a student’s university choice by guaranteeing otherwise uncertain admission into competitive majors. Schools may or may not benefit from the direct admissions equilibrium - as opposed to one with no direct admissions - based on signal strength and school quality.
